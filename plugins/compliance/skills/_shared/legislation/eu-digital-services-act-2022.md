# EU Digital Services Act 2022 (Regulation (EU) 2022/2065)
> **Source**: https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX%3A32022R2065
> **Compiled**: From training knowledge (August 2025 cutoff) — verify against current official text
> **Enforcement body**: Digital Services Coordinators (DSCs) in each EU member state; European Commission (for VLOPs/VLOSEs); European Board for Digital Services
> **Scope**: Intermediary services (including hosting services, online platforms, search engines) offered to EU recipients; tiered obligations based on service type and size

## Overview

The Digital Services Act (DSA) is an EU regulation that establishes a harmonised framework of obligations for providers of intermediary services in the EU. It replaces the liability provisions of the e-Commerce Directive (2000/31/EC) and introduces tiered due diligence obligations that scale with the size and risk profile of the provider.

The DSA applies extraterritorially — non-EU providers must comply if they offer services to recipients in the EU (i.e., if EU users can access the service).

---

## Provider Categories

The DSA uses a layered structure. Each higher category inherits obligations from lower categories:

1. **Intermediary services** (all in scope): Mere conduit, caching, hosting services.
2. **Hosting services**: Store information at the request of a recipient.
3. **Online platforms**: Hosting services that disseminate information to the public (social media, app stores, marketplaces, content platforms).
4. **Very Large Online Platforms (VLOPs)**: Online platforms with ≥45 million average monthly active EU recipients, designated by the Commission.
5. **Very Large Online Search Engines (VLOSEs)**: Search engines with ≥45 million average monthly active EU recipients.

---

## Chapter I — General Provisions

### Article 2 — Definitions (selected)

- **Intermediary service**: Mere conduit, caching, or hosting services.
- **Online platform**: A hosting service that, at the request of a recipient, stores and disseminates information to the public (unless this is a minor/ancillary feature of another service).
- **Recipient**: Any person using an intermediary service (including businesses and consumers).
- **Consumer**: Natural person acting for purposes outside trade/business/profession.
- **Illegal content**: Content that is, by itself or by its reference to an activity (including the sale of products/services), not in compliance with EU law or with the law of a member state (consistent with EU law).
- **Systemic risk**: Risks arising from VLOPs/VLOSEs' reach, network effects, and algorithmic amplification.

### Article 3 — Country of Establishment

Providers established in the EU are supervised by the DSC of their member state of main establishment. Non-EU providers must designate a legal representative in a member state.

## Chapter II — Liability Exemptions (Articles 4–8)

The DSA preserves and clarifies the "safe harbour" liability exemptions from the e-Commerce Directive:

### Article 4 — Mere Conduit
No liability for information transmitted by a provider that does not initiate, select the receiver, or select/modify transmitted information.

### Article 5 — Caching
No liability for automatic, intermediate, temporary storage of information (caching), provided the provider does not modify, complies with conditions of access, and expeditiously removes/disables access to information when notified of illegality.

### Article 6 — Hosting
No liability for stored information provided by a recipient if the provider:
- Has no actual knowledge of illegal activity/content; **or**
- Upon obtaining knowledge or awareness, acts expeditiously to remove or disable access.

The exemption does not apply when the recipient acts under the authority/control of the provider.

### Article 8 — No General Monitoring Obligation
Member states must not impose a general obligation on providers to monitor stored information, nor a general obligation to actively seek illegal content.

## Chapter III — Due Diligence Obligations

### Section 1 — All Intermediary Services

#### Article 11 — Points of Contact
Providers must designate a single point of contact for member state authorities and the Commission, accessible electronically.

#### Article 12 — Legal Representative (non-EU providers)
Providers established outside the EU that offer services in the EU must designate a legal representative in a member state.

#### Article 13 — Transparency Reports
Providers must publish at least annual transparency reports on content moderation activities, including: number of orders received, notices received, content moderation measures taken, use of automated tools, and complaints handled.

---

### Section 2 — Hosting Services

#### Article 16 — Notice and Action Mechanisms
Hosting services must provide an easily accessible, user-friendly mechanism for any person or entity to submit **notices** (flagging illegal content). Notices must include: URL, explanation, statement of good faith belief that content is illegal, contact details.

#### Article 17 — Statement of Reasons
When a provider removes/restricts access to content, or suspends/restricts a user's account, it must provide a **clear and specific statement of reasons** to the affected recipient including: the factual and legal basis, the scope, the duration, and available redress options.

#### Article 18 — Notification to Law Enforcement
If a hosting service has information giving rise to a **suspicion of a criminal offence involving a threat to life or safety**, it must promptly notify law enforcement or judicial authorities in the relevant member state.

---

### Section 3 — Online Platforms

#### Article 19 — Exemption for Small Providers
Micro and small enterprises (fewer than 50 staff; annual turnover or balance sheet ≤ €10 million) are exempt from Articles 20–28 (complaints, out-of-court settlement, trusted flaggers, counter-notices, misuse measures, transparency of advertising, recommender system transparency). Exception: VLOP-designated providers regardless of size.

#### Article 20 — Internal Complaint-Handling Systems
Online platforms must provide an effective internal complaint-handling system allowing recipients to lodge complaints (free of charge, electronically) about:
- Decisions to remove, restrict, or demote content.
- Decisions to suspend/terminate user accounts.
- Decisions to restrict ability to monetise content.

The platform must handle complaints in a timely, non-discriminatory, diligent, and non-arbitrary manner, and notify complainants of decisions with reasons.

#### Article 21 — Out-of-Court Dispute Settlement
Users must have the right to refer disputes (unresolved after internal complaint) to certified **out-of-court dispute settlement bodies** (certified by the DSC). Providers must engage in good faith and be bound by the settlement if agreed.

#### Article 22 — Trusted Flaggers
DSCs may certify **trusted flaggers** (specialised bodies with expertise in detecting illegal content in specific areas). Notices from trusted flaggers must be processed with priority. Providers must publish data on the number of trusted flagger notices received and actioned.

#### Article 23 — Measures Against Misuse
Online platforms must suspend (for a reasonable period after warning) users who frequently provide manifestly illegal content, or who frequently submit manifestly unfounded notices or complaints (misuse of the reporting system).

#### Article 24 — Transparency of Advertising
Online platforms that display advertising must ensure that for each advertisement displayed, the recipient can identify:
- That it is an advertisement (clearly labelled).
- On whose behalf it is displayed.
- The principal parameters used to target them with that specific advertisement.
- Meaningful information about the main parameters used.

#### Article 25 — Recommender System Transparency
Online platforms that use recommender systems must set out in plain language the main parameters used, and why. At least one option must be offered **not based on profiling**.

#### Article 26 — Advertising Prohibition for Certain Targeting
Providers must not display targeted advertising based on profiling using:
- Special categories of personal data (Article 9 GDPR).
- Profiling of minors.

#### Article 27 — Transparency of Recommender Systems (Accessibility)
Providers must provide a readily accessible and reliable means to identify the main parameters of recommender systems and to modify or influence them.

#### Article 28 — Online Protection of Minors

**This is one of the most important DSA provisions for platforms accessible to minors.**

28(1): Online platforms accessible to minors must put in place appropriate and proportionate measures to ensure a **high level of privacy, safety, and security** for minors on their service.

28(2): Online platforms **must not** display advertising to recipients that they know (or have reasonable grounds to know) are minors, **based on profiling** using their personal data.

28(3): Compliance with Article 28 does not require the platform to process additional personal data solely to assess whether a user is a minor.

**Practical implications**:
- No behavioural/profiling-based advertising to known or suspected minors.
- Safety-by-default for minors (default privacy settings, safety features on).
- "Appropriate and proportionate" age assurance may be required to give effect to this article.
- This applies to **all online platforms**, not just VLOPs.

### Section 4 — Very Large Online Platforms (VLOPs) and VLOSEs

#### Article 33 — Designation
The Commission designates VLOPs and VLOSEs with ≥45 million average monthly active EU recipients. Providers must report their user numbers annually. The Commission publishes the list of designated entities.

#### Article 34 — Risk Assessment

Designated VLOPs/VLOSEs must conduct thorough annual risk assessments of:
- **Systemic risks** arising from the design, features, or functioning of the service in the EU, including:
  - Dissemination of illegal content.
  - Adverse effects on fundamental rights (privacy, freedom of expression, non-discrimination).
  - Actual or foreseeable negative effects on civic discourse and electoral processes.
  - Actual or foreseeable negative effects on **public health**, **gender-based violence**, **protection of minors**, and **mental health**.
- The risk assessment must consider: algorithmic systems, content moderation, terms of service, advertising systems, data access practices.

#### Article 35 — Risk Mitigation Measures

VLOPs/VLOSEs must implement **reasonable, proportionate, and effective** mitigation measures to address risks identified under Article 34. These may include:

- Adapting content moderation or recommender systems.
- Applying safety-by-default settings (particularly for minors).
- Providing users with more effective tools to flag illegal content.
- Cooperating with trusted flaggers.
- Providing media literacy tools.
- Taking targeted measures to protect minor users (e.g., restricting recommender systems for minors).
- Putting in place safety measures for minors' access to certain content categories.

#### Article 36 — Crisis Response
In the event of a formally designated crisis (affecting public security or public health), the Commission may require specific VLOPs/VLOSEs to take emergency measures.

#### Article 37 — Independent Audit
VLOPs/VLOSEs must commission at least annual **independent audits** of their compliance with the DSA. Audit results must be transmitted to the DSC and the Commission.

#### Article 38 — Recommender System Choice (VLOPs)
VLOPs using recommender systems must offer at least one option not based on profiling and ensure that the alternative is as prominent as profiling-based options.

#### Article 39 — Advertising Repository (VLOPs)
VLOPs must maintain a publicly accessible searchable repository of all advertisements displayed on their service, with information on targeting parameters, reach, and duration (for at least 1 year after last display).

#### Article 40 — Data Access for Researchers
VLOPs/VLOSEs must provide vetted researchers with access to data necessary to understand systemic risks. The Commission oversees the vetting process.

#### Article 42 — Transparency Reporting (VLOPs/VLOSEs)
Enhanced transparency reports required at least every 6 months (vs. annually for others), including data on the risk assessments, audits, and mitigation measures.

## Enforcement

### National Digital Services Coordinators (Articles 49–51)
Each EU member state designates a DSC with powers to investigate, issue orders, and fine providers established in their territory.

### European Board for Digital Services (Articles 61–66)
A body composed of DSCs from all member states that coordinates enforcement, issues opinions, and facilitates cross-border cases.

### Commission Enforcement (VLOPs/VLOSEs) (Articles 74–76)
The Commission has direct enforcement power over designated VLOPs and VLOSEs.

### Fines (Article 74)
| Violation | Maximum fine |
|---|---|
| Non-compliance with DSA obligations | 6% of worldwide annual turnover |
| Providing incorrect/misleading/incomplete information | 1% of worldwide annual turnover |
| Periodic penalty payments | Up to 5% of average daily worldwide turnover per day |

### Interim Measures (Article 70)
The Commission may order interim measures against a VLOP/VLOSE in cases of urgency (serious risk of grave damage).

## Interaction with GDPR

The DSA explicitly states that it does not override GDPR. Where DSA provisions relate to personal data processing (e.g., profiling for advertising), GDPR also applies. Providers must comply with both.

Key overlaps:
- Article 28 DSA (minors) + Article 8 GDPR (children's consent).
- Article 26 DSA (no profiling-based ads to minors) + legitimate interests / special categories under GDPR.
- Risk assessments under Article 34 DSA complement Data Protection Impact Assessments under Article 35 GDPR.
