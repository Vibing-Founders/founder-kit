# Ofcom: Children's Risk Assessment Guidance and Children's Risk Profiles

> **Source**: Ofcom, *Children's Risk Assessment Guidance and Children's Risk Profiles*, published 24 April 2025.
> **Full document**: https://www.ofcom.org.uk/siteassets/resources/documents/consultations/category-1-10-weeks/statement-protecting-children-from-harms-online/main-document/childrens-risk-assessment-guidance-and-childrens-risk-profiles.pdf
>
> This is Ofcom's statutory guidance under the UK Online Safety Act 2023. Services must carry out a children's risk assessment (CRA) if they are user-to-user services or search services that are "likely to be accessed by children". The CRA must be "suitable and sufficient".

## 1. Who must comply

The children's risk assessment duty applies to:

- **User-to-user (U2U) services** regulated under the OSA that are "likely to be accessed by children" — the duty is in **s.11 of the Act**
- **Search services** regulated under the OSA that are "likely to be accessed by children" — the duty is in **s.28 of the Act**

"Likely to be accessed by children" is determined by a separate children's access assessment (CAA). If your CAA concludes children are likely to access your service, the CRA duty applies.

The CRA duty applies **in addition** to the illegal content risk assessment duty (s.9 for U2U, s.26 for search), not instead of it. Category 1 services have additional duties under s.14.

---

## 2. What "suitable and sufficient" means

Ofcom sets a **suitability standard** — the CRA must be proportionate to the size, nature, and risk profile of the service. Larger services with more risk factors are expected to do more.

### Record-keeping

You must keep a written record of your CRA. The record must be kept up to date and made available to Ofcom on request. There is no prescribed format.

### Timing

The first CRA must be completed by the deadline set out in Schedule 3 of the Act (Ofcom issues codes of practice with specific dates). After that, the CRA must be kept up to date and a new one carried out:
- When Ofcom significantly changes a risk profile that relates to services of your kind
- Before making any **significant change** to the service (see section 8)

## 3. Content harmful to children: taxonomy

The OSA distinguishes three categories. You must assess all three.

### Primary priority content (4 kinds — highest scrutiny)

| Kind | Description |
|---|---|
| CSAM and sexual content involving minors | Content that sexually exploits children (whether depicting real or AI-generated children) |
| Content that encourages, promotes, or provides instructions for suicide or self-harm | Includes content that glorifies or normalises these acts |
| Content that encourages, promotes, or provides instructions for eating disorders or behaviours associated with eating disorders | |
| Pornographic content | Content that is of a nature that would be prohibited for sale to under-18s under UK law |

### Priority content (8 kinds)

| Kind | Description |
|---|---|
| Violent content | Content depicting serious violence, including content that may distress or desensitise |
| Bullying content | Content targeting an individual with the aim of causing distress |
| Content that is racist, antisemitic, or otherwise discriminatory | |
| Content that may have a negative effect on a child's body image | Not limited to eating disorders — includes content promoting unrealistic body standards |
| Content that encourages, promotes, or facilitates dangerous activities | Including challenges and stunts |
| Content that depicts or facilitates the sexual abuse of children | A broader category than primary priority CSAM — includes grooming, contextually sexual content |
| Content that facilitates offline harms or criminal activity involving children | |
| Content promoting negative mental health outcomes | Depression, hopelessness, despair — "depression content" |

### Non-designated content

Any content that is harmful to children but does not fall into the above categories. You must:
1. Identify whether any such content exists on your service
2. Assess the risk it poses to children
3. **Notify Ofcom** if you identify the presence of such content — this is a specific legal obligation

## 4. The four-step CRA methodology

### Step 1: Identify content harmful to children your service presents

Use Ofcom's **Children's Risk Profiles** (see section 5) to identify which kinds of content harmful to children your service could present. For each kind, consider:
- Whether the content is possible on your service given its design and functionality
- Which age groups of children are at risk
- What risk factors from the Risk Profiles apply to your service

### Step 2: Assess the risk level for each kind of content

For each kind of content identified in Step 1, assess the risk level using the **Risk Level Table** (see section 7): High, Medium, Low, or Negligible/No risk.

Risk is a function of **likelihood** (how likely is a child to encounter this content on your service?) and **impact** (how severe would the harm be if they did?).

Evidence inputs (section 6) inform this assessment.

**When evidence is not conclusive, err on the side of caution and select the higher risk level.**

### Step 3: Decide what safety measures to implement

Based on your risk levels, select and implement proportionate safety measures. The OSA Protection of Children Codes of Practice set out the specific measures Ofcom expects at different risk levels.

The measures must be designed to:
- Prevent children from encountering primary priority content
- Protect children from encountering priority content
- Mitigate the risk of non-designated content where identified

### Step 4: Record, report, and review

- Record your assessment in writing
- Notify Ofcom of any non-designated content you identify
- Keep your assessment up to date
- Review it when Ofcom updates risk profiles or when you make a significant change to your service

## 5. Children's Risk Profiles: risk factors

Ofcom publishes Children's Risk Profiles which list the specific risk factors that increase the likelihood of particular kinds of content appearing on a service, and the factors that affect impact. These are used in Step 1 of the CRA.

### User-to-user service risk factors (examples by content area)

The Risk Profiles are structured as tables listing risk factors for each kind of content. Key cross-cutting risk factors include:

**Functionalities that increase risk:**
- Recommender systems / algorithmic content feeds (especially autoplay)
- Search and discovery functionality (allows users to actively find harmful content)
- Direct or private messaging between users
- Livestreaming functionality
- Anonymous or pseudonymous user profiles
- Ability to share, reshare, or remix content (amplification potential)
- Group chat with non-connected users
- Content that can go viral or be widely disseminated rapidly

**User characteristics that increase risk:**
- Children constitute a significant proportion of the user base
- Service is particularly popular with younger teens (13–15) or pre-teens
- Service actively targets children as a primary audience
- Service design has features strongly appealing to children

**Governance factors that increase risk:**
- No or minimal age assurance (reliance on self-declared age only)
- No or weak content moderation (automated or human)
- Terms of service that do not prohibit content harmful to children
- No or limited user reporting mechanisms
- No effective systems to detect or remove harmful content

**Content environment factors:**
- High volume of user-generated content
- Content environment where harmful content is common on similar services
- Platform used in contexts associated with higher risk (e.g., nightlife, adult social networks)

### Children's Search Risk Profiles

For search services, risk factors relate to:
- Whether the service indexes content sources that commonly include harmful content
- How the service ranks, surfaces, and auto-suggests results
- Whether the service has safe search functionality and how effective it is
- Whether children can easily search for terms associated with harmful content

### Non-designated content

Non-designated content is harmful content that falls outside the defined categories above. You must assess your service for the presence of this kind of content as part of your CRA. Examples include:
- Content promoting extreme political views that could harm children's wellbeing
- Certain types of violent extremist content not covered by illegal content duties
- Trends or challenges that are harmful but not covered by the priority categories

If you identify such content, you must notify Ofcom — specifying the kind of content and its incidence on the service.

## 6. Evidence inputs: core and enhanced

### Core inputs (all services must consider)

| Input | What to gather |
|---|---|
| **Prevalence of content** | Complaints received, user reports, content moderation data, trusted flagger referrals, law enforcement referrals. Do not rely solely on complaint volume — low complaints may reflect underreporting rather than low prevalence. |
| **User data including age insights** | Account data, registration data, behavioural data, age estimation/verification outputs. Self-declared age alone is not sufficient — Ofcom expects you to assume the true number of children could be significantly higher than suggested by self-declared data. |
| **Retrospective analysis of incidents of harm** | Following any significant incident, conduct a "lessons learned" analysis to inform the CRA. |
| **Findings of your illegal content risk assessment** | Elements of the illegal content assessment (features, functionalities, user base) are also relevant to the children's CRA — some illegal content overlaps with content harmful to children (e.g. suicide-related content is both priority illegal content and primary priority children's content). |
| **Findings of your children's access assessment** | Helps you understand the composition of your child user base. |
| **Evidence from existing controls** | Results of existing content moderation systems, age assurance processes, and other risk-reduction measures — to assess their effectiveness in reducing children's exposure. |
| **Other relevant information** | User reporting on content, negative signals from children about suggested content, research from expert bodies, product testing outcomes, other audits or assessments. |

### Enhanced inputs

Enhanced inputs are **expected for large services (those with more than 7 million monthly UK users)** and for services that have identified several specific risk factors, or where core inputs alone are insufficient to assess risk accurately.

| Enhanced input | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **Results of product testing** | Run tests on features before full deployment (including A/B tests on recommender systems) to understand the impact on children's safety. Particularly relevant for new features or AI-driven features. |
| **Consultation with internal experts on risks and safety measures** | Internal child protection or child safety specialists, child development experts. Should happen regularly and feed into the annual review cycle. |
| **Views of independent external experts** | External child safety experts, academic researchers, regulatory standards experts. Validates and challenges your internal assessment. |
| **Internal and external commissioned research** | Commission research into specific risk areas relevant to your service — e.g., how a specific content category manifests on your service, or child behavioural patterns on the platform. |
| **Outcomes of external audit or risk assurance processes** | Third-party audits of trust and safety processes. Particularly useful where services lack in-house capacity to self-assure, or want independent scrutiny. |
| **Consultation with users and user research** | Research with child users (or their parents/guardians) to understand their experience of harmful content and the usability of safety features. Can be platform-wide or targeted at specific demographics (e.g. children with vulnerabilities). |
| **Engaging with representative groups** | Outreach to organisations representing specific groups of children (e.g. children with disabilities, children from specific demographics) — particularly useful when a service change is likely to disproportionately affect a group. |

## 7. Risk Level Table

Used in Step 2 to assign a risk level to each kind of content harmful to children. Risk is assessed on two dimensions: **likelihood** and **impact**. The final risk level should reflect both. Where they diverge, make a holistic judgement — erring on the side of caution.

| Risk level | Likelihood conditions | Impact conditions |
|---|---|---|
| **High** | Significant evidence of this content being or likely being encountered (e.g., from complaints, external experts). **Or** many specific risk factors from the Children's Risk Profiles apply, with no effective systems to address them. | Evidence of (1) a substantial amount of content being present, **or** (2) actual harm to many children. **Or** content is known to have severe impact even if affecting few children (e.g. very vulnerable group). **Or** service has over 1 million monthly UK child users, or very high frequency/time of use by children, or actively targets children as primary audience with content strongly appealing to children. |
| **Medium** | Evidence of content being (or likely being) encountered. **Or** several risk factors which increase likelihood, with some systems in place but unable to demonstrate effectiveness. **Or** service does not prohibit this content and has some systems but cannot demonstrate they reduce risks. | Evidence of (1) a moderate amount of content, **or** (2) actual harm to a moderate number of children. **Or** content would have moderate impact. **Or** service has between 100,000 and 1 million monthly UK child users, or high frequency/time of use, or content/features strongly appealing to children. |
| **Low** | No evidence of this content, or few/no specific risk factors, or comprehensive systems in place that demonstrably reduce the likelihood (e.g., all content is pre-moderated and no such content has ever appeared). | Occasional examples of content with limited potential to affect children — impacts few child users and severity is low. |
| **Negligible / No risk** | Evidence shows it is not possible or extremely unlikely that children could encounter this content on the service. | — |

**Key calibration notes:**
- A service that *prohibits* content may still be High or Medium risk for that content — prohibition alone is not sufficient if there are no effective enforcement systems.
- Having the *potential* to impact a large number of children is not, by itself, a strong indicator of High risk, especially absent evidence of content being present. But a service at the scale thresholds above needs robust evidence to demonstrate it is not High or Medium risk.
- If you have evidence of a substantial amount of harmful content on your service, Ofcom would **normally expect you to assess as High risk** for that content type.

## 8. Making a significant change

**You must carry out a new CRA before making a significant change to any aspect of your service's design or operation that is reasonably likely to have a significant impact on the risk of harm to children.** This is a specific legal duty.

### Changes likely to require a new CRA

A change is likely to be **significant** if one or more of the following apply:

- It alters the risk factors you identified in your last CRA
- It materially impacts a substantial proportion of your child user base (age groups or volume of children)
- It materially impacts a vulnerable user group (e.g., children in a specific age group or with specific characteristics)
- It materially impacts the efficacy of the safety measures you have in place
- It materially impacts your revenue model, growth strategy, or ownership in a way that affects service design

**Questions to ask about the proposed change:**
- Would it materially impact children's experience or behaviour in a way that may affect risks?
- Does it affect the ability or incentives of users to create or share content harmful to children?
- Will it affect child user reporting abilities (e.g. changes to the UI or reporting flows)?
- Does it add new functionalities or enable children to interact differently?
- Does it affect content or network recommendations?
- Does it involve a new or different content moderation process?
- Does it change how revenue is generated or the growth strategy?

### Specific examples of significant changes

- **Recommender system changes**: introduction of a new recommender, new ML model within an existing recommender (especially if it changes age-related predictions), changing the goal criteria of a recommender (e.g., to maximise watch time), on-platform testing that reveals significant safety impacts
- **Adding or removing functionalities**: adding direct messaging, livestreaming, end-to-end encryption, sharing/resharing, generative AI features, or autoplay — or removing controls on these
- **Changes to platform content rules**: altering what content is prohibited or how content is prioritised
- **UI changes affecting safety features**: changes to the location, prominence, wording, or comprehensibility of reporting functions or safety notifications
- **Introducing or changing prompts**: alerts or reminders about content settings — timing and language can affect children's choices
- **Acquisitions**: integrating functionality from an acquired service, or migrating child users
- **Changes in ownership or investment** that may influence how the service operates
- **Revenue model changes**: new revenue streams or significant changes to KPIs that affect service design
- **Growth strategy changes**: that affect the speed of child user growth or the composition of age groups
- **Changes in headcount** that reduce the capacity to assess or mitigate risk to children

**Note**: A very minor or routine change (e.g., a bug fix, visual tweak that does not affect safety features) does not require a new CRA. The significance test is about impact on risk to children.

---

## 9. Record-keeping and review

- Keep a written record of your CRA at all times (no prescribed format)
- The record must cover all four steps of the methodology
- Make the record available to Ofcom on request
- Review and update the CRA:
  - When Ofcom updates a risk profile relating to your kind of service
  - Before any significant change to the service
  - Following any significant incident of harm to children
  - On a regular cycle (Ofcom guidance suggests at least annually for most services)
- If you identify non-designated content harmful to children, notify Ofcom promptly, specifying the kind of content and its incidence

## 10. Appendix: Comparison with illegal content risk assessment duties

The children's CRA duty (s.11 / s.28) applies **in addition to** the illegal content risk assessment duty (s.9 / s.26). The two assessments share a similar structure but differ in important ways:

### Key differences in the children's CRA

| Aspect | Illegal content RA | Children's RA |
|---|---|---|
| User base assessment | Assess user base generally | Must assess **number of users who are children in different age groups** |
| Content categories | Priority illegal content + other illegal content | Primary priority + priority + non-designated content, each separately assessed |
| Age-group consideration | General harm to individuals | Must give **separate consideration to children in different age groups** throughout |
| Functionalities of special concern | Facilitating illegal content or offences generally | **Functionalities enabling adults to search for or contact children** specifically identified |
| Usage patterns | How the service is used generally | Must consider features that affect **how much children use the service** (e.g., autoplay) |
| Non-designated content | No equivalent | Must identify and **notify Ofcom** if non-designated content is present |
| Additional obligations for Category 1 services | s.23 record-keeping duties | Also s.14 additional duties for Category 1 services likely accessed by children |

### Statutory scope (user-to-user)
- **Illegal content**: s.9 — applies to all regulated U2U services
- **Children's**: s.11 — applies to regulated U2U services likely to be accessed by children (in addition to s.9)

### Statutory scope (search)
- **Illegal content**: s.26 — applies to all regulated search services
- **Children's**: s.28 — applies to regulated search services likely to be accessed by children (in addition to s.26)

## Quick reference: What Ofcom expects at a glance

| You are... | Ofcom expects... |
|---|---|
| A small service (under 100K monthly UK child users) with few risk factors | Core inputs only; straightforward four-step assessment; written record |
| A medium service (100K–1M monthly UK child users) or with several risk factors | Core inputs; enhanced inputs for specific high-risk content areas; more detailed risk assessment |
| A large service (over 7M monthly UK users) | Core inputs **plus** enhanced inputs across the board; external expert consultation; product testing; regular audit cycle |
| Any service, on making a significant change | New CRA covering the impacts of the proposed change, before the change goes live |
| Any service that finds non-designated content | Notify Ofcom (kind of content + incidence) as a legal obligation |
| Any service where evidence is ambiguous | **Default to the higher risk level** |
