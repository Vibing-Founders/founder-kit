## Overview

The EU GDPR is the primary data protection regulation in the European Union and European Economic Area (EEA). It applies directly in all EU member states and has no separate implementing legislation (unlike directives). It entered into force on 24 May 2016 and became fully applicable on 25 May 2018.

For non-EU entities, the "establishment" or "offering goods/services to" or "monitoring behaviour of" tests in Article 3(2) create extraterritorial obligations. Non-EU controllers/processors must appoint an EU representative (Article 27).

## Key Recitals

Recitals are not legally binding but are used by courts and DPAs to interpret the operative articles.

**Recital 32 — Consent**:
Consent should be given by a clear affirmative act establishing a freely given, specific, informed, and unambiguous indication of the data subject's agreement. Silence, pre-ticked boxes, or inactivity does not constitute consent. Consent should cover all processing activities carried out for the same purpose. Where multiple purposes exist, separate consent should be given for each.

**Recital 38 — Children**:
Children merit specific protection with regard to their personal data, as they may be less aware of the risks, consequences, and safeguards concerned and their rights in relation to the processing of personal data. Such specific protection should apply when using personal data of children for marketing purposes or creating personality or user profiles, and the collection of personal data in relation to children when using services offered directly to a child. The consent of the holder of parental responsibility should not be necessary in the context of preventive or counselling services offered directly to a child.

**Recital 47 — Legitimate Interests**:
The legitimate interests of a controller may provide a legal basis for processing where the legitimate interests are not overridden by the interests or fundamental rights and freedoms of the data subject. Such legitimate interest could exist where there is a relevant and appropriate relationship between the data subject and the controller (e.g., customer relationships). The processing of personal data strictly necessary for the purposes of preventing fraud also constitutes a legitimate interest. **The existence of a legitimate interest would need careful assessment including whether a data subject can reasonably expect at the time and in the context of the collection of the personal data that processing for that purpose may take place.**

**Recital 48 — Legitimate Interests within Group of Undertakings**:
Controllers that are part of a group of undertakings or institutions affiliated to a central body may have a legitimate interest in transmitting personal data within the group of undertakings for internal administrative purposes.

**Recital 49 — Network Security**:
Processing of personal data to the extent strictly necessary and proportionate for the purposes of ensuring network and information security constitutes a legitimate interest.

**Recital 71 — Automated Decision-Making and Profiling**:
The data subject should have the right not to be subject to a decision, which may include a measure, evaluating personal aspects relating to him or her which is based solely on automated processing. In any case, such processing should be subject to suitable safeguards, including specific information to the data subject and the right to obtain human intervention, to express his or her point of view, to obtain an explanation of the decision reached after such assessment, and to challenge the decision. Such a measure should not concern a child.

**Recital 75 — Risk Assessment**:
The risk to the rights and freedoms of natural persons, of varying likelihood and severity, may result from personal data processing which could lead to physical, material, or non-material damage — in particular where the processing may give rise to discrimination, identity theft or fraud, financial loss, damage to the reputation, loss of confidentiality of data protected by professional secrecy, unauthorised reversal of pseudonymisation, or any other significant economic or social disadvantage. Where the data subject might be deprived of their rights and freedoms, or prevented from exercising control over their personal data. **This risk is particularly high when processing involves children.**

## Article 3 — Territorial Scope

**Article 3(1)**: Applies to processing of personal data in the context of activities of an establishment of a controller or processor in the EU, regardless of whether the processing takes place in the EU.

**Article 3(2)**: Applies to processing of personal data of data subjects who are in the EU by a controller/processor not established in the EU, where the processing activities relate to:
- (a) **Offering goods or services** (whether for payment or not) to data subjects in the EU; or
- (b) **Monitoring of behaviour** of data subjects as far as their behaviour takes place within the EU.

**Article 3(3)**: Applies to processing by non-EU controllers in a place where EU member state law applies by virtue of public international law.

---

## Article 4 — Definitions (Key)

- **Personal data**: Any information relating to an identified or identifiable natural person. Includes online identifiers (IP addresses, cookie IDs, device identifiers), location data.
- **Processing**: Any operation on personal data (collection, storage, adaptation, retrieval, use, disclosure, erasure, etc.).
- **Pseudonymisation**: Processing personal data such that data can no longer be attributed to a specific individual without additional information, kept separately with technical/organisational measures.
- **Controller**: Entity that determines purposes and means of processing.
- **Processor**: Entity that processes on behalf of a controller.
- **Third party**: Any person other than the data subject, controller, processor, or persons under direct authority of the controller/processor.
- **Consent**: Freely given, specific, informed, and unambiguous indication by statement or clear affirmative action.
- **Personal data breach**: Breach of security leading to accidental or unlawful destruction, loss, alteration, unauthorised disclosure, or access to personal data.
- **Genetic data**: Personal data relating to inherited or acquired genetic characteristics giving unique information about physiology/health.
- **Biometric data**: Personal data resulting from specific technical processing relating to physical/physiological/behavioural characteristics allowing/confirming unique identification (e.g., facial images, fingerprint data).
- **Health data**: Personal data related to physical or mental health of a natural person, including provision of health care services.
- **Profiling**: Any form of automated processing to evaluate personal aspects, particularly to analyse/predict performance, economic situation, health, preferences, interests, reliability, behaviour, location, or movements.

## Article 5 — Principles Relating to Processing of Personal Data

1. **Lawfulness, fairness, transparency**
2. **Purpose limitation** — specified, explicit, legitimate purposes; no further incompatible processing
3. **Data minimisation** — adequate, relevant, limited to what is necessary
4. **Accuracy** — kept up to date; reasonable steps to erase/rectify
5. **Storage limitation** — no longer than necessary; longer storage permitted for archiving in public interest, scientific/historical research, statistical purposes with appropriate safeguards
6. **Integrity and confidentiality** — appropriate security (including protection against unauthorised/unlawful processing, accidental loss, destruction, damage)

**Article 5(2) — Accountability**: Controller responsible for and must be able to demonstrate compliance with all principles.

---

## Article 6 — Lawfulness of Processing

| Basis | Summary |
|---|---|
| **(a) Consent** | Freely given, specific, informed, unambiguous indication |
| **(b) Contract** | Necessary for contract performance or pre-contractual steps at request of data subject |
| **(c) Legal obligation** | Necessary for compliance with legal obligation of controller |
| **(d) Vital interests** | Necessary to protect vital interests of data subject or another person |
| **(e) Public task** | Necessary for performance of a task in the public interest or exercise of official authority |
| **(f) Legitimate interests** | Necessary for legitimate interests of controller or third party, not overridden by data subject's interests or fundamental rights |

For special categories, additional conditions under Article 9 must be met.

---

## Article 7 — Conditions for Consent

- Controller must demonstrate that consent was given (burden of proof on controller).
- If consent is part of a written declaration covering other matters, it must be clearly distinguishable from other matters, in an intelligible and accessible form, using clear and plain language. Any part not compliant shall not be binding.
- Data subject has the right to withdraw consent at any time. Withdrawal must be as easy as giving consent. Withdrawal does not affect lawfulness of processing prior to withdrawal.
- Assessing whether consent is freely given: take utmost account of whether performance of a contract is made conditional on consent to processing not necessary for that contract.

---

## Article 8 — Conditions Applicable to Child's Consent for Information Society Services

**Article 8(1)**: Where Article 6(1)(a) (consent) is the lawful basis and an information society service is offered directly to a child:
- Processing is lawful where the child is **at least 16** years of age.
- Member states may lower this threshold to a minimum of **13 years**.
- For children below the applicable age, processing is lawful only if and to the extent consent is given or authorised by the holder of parental responsibility.

**Article 8(2)**: The controller must make **reasonable efforts** to verify that consent is given or authorised by the holder of parental responsibility, taking into account available technology.

**Article 8(3)**: This article does not affect general contract law of member states such as rules on the validity, formation, or effect of a contract in relation to a child.

## Article 9 — Processing of Special Categories of Personal Data

**Prohibition**: Processing of racial/ethnic origin, political opinions, religious/philosophical beliefs, trade union membership, genetic data, biometric data (for unique identification), health data, sex life/sexual orientation is **prohibited** unless an exception in Article 9(2) applies:

**(a)** Explicit consent of the data subject (where not prohibited by member state law).
**(b)** Employment, social security, social protection law obligations.
**(c)** Vital interests where the data subject is physically/legally incapable of giving consent.
**(d)** Legitimate activities of foundations, associations, etc. with a political, philosophical, religious, or trade-union aim — relating to members/former members/those in regular contact.
**(e)** Data manifestly made public by the data subject.
**(f)** Necessary for establishment, exercise, or defence of legal claims; judicial acting in judicial capacity.
**(g)** Substantial public interest, on basis of EU or member state law, proportionate to aim, essential protections in place.
**(h)** Preventive/occupational medicine, medical diagnosis, health/social care — subject to professional secrecy.
**(i)** Public health (serious cross-border health threats) — with appropriate safeguards.
**(j)** Archiving in public interest, scientific/historical research, statistics — subject to appropriate safeguards.

## Articles 12–14 — Transparency (Right to Information)

### Article 12 — Transparent Information
Privacy information must be concise, transparent, intelligible, easily accessible, and in clear, plain language. Provided free of charge. In writing or by other means (including electronic). When requested by data subject, information may be provided orally where identity confirmed.

### Article 13 — Information at Point of Collection (Direct Collection)
At the time of collection, provide:
- Controller and DPO contact details.
- Purposes and legal basis for each processing activity.
- Legitimate interests (if LI basis).
- Recipients or categories.
- International transfers and safeguards.
- Retention periods (or criteria).
- All data subject rights.
- Right to withdraw consent.
- Right to lodge complaint with supervisory authority.
- Automated decision-making, logic involved, significance, and envisaged consequences.

### Article 14 — Information Where Data Not Collected Directly
Provide within 1 month of obtaining data (or at first communication, or when first disclosed to third party, whichever earlier). Additionally include: categories of data; source; whether from publicly accessible sources.

## Articles 15–22 — Rights of Data Subjects

| Article | Right | Key Details |
|---|---|---|
| **15** | Access | Confirmation; copy of data; information on processing; supplementary information as per Articles 13/14 |
| **16** | Rectification | Correct inaccurate personal data; complete incomplete data |
| **17** | Erasure | Right to be forgotten — grounds: no longer necessary; consent withdrawn; objection upheld; unlawful processing; legal obligation; child's data collected under Art. 8 |
| **18** | Restriction | Limit processing to storage only — pending accuracy challenge; unlawful but restriction preferred to erasure; controller no longer needs data but subject needs for legal claims; pending Article 21 objection |
| **19** | Notification obligation | Notify recipients of rectification/erasure/restriction |
| **20** | Data portability | Machine-readable format; direct transmission to another controller (where technically feasible); applies to consent or contract basis + automated processing |
| **21** | Object | Object to LI/public task processing incl. profiling; absolute right to object to direct marketing profiling; object to processing for research/statistics on grounds relating to particular situation |
| **22** | Automated decisions | No solely automated decision producing legal/similarly significant effects, unless: contract; authorised by law; explicit consent (+ human review right + explanation + ability to contest) — **must not be applied to children** |

Response timeline: Within **1 calendar month** of receipt. Extendable by 2 further months for complex/numerous requests (with notice of extension within first month).

## Article 25 — Data Protection by Design and by Default

**By design**: Controller must implement appropriate technical and organisational measures designed to implement the data protection principles efficiently. Must be done both at the time of determining the means of processing and at the time of processing itself.

**By default**: Controller must implement measures ensuring that, by default, only personal data that is necessary for each specific purpose is processed. Specifically regarding: amount of data collected, extent of processing, period of storage, accessibility.

---

## Article 35 — Data Protection Impact Assessment

**Mandatory when**: Processing is likely to result in a high risk to the rights and freedoms of natural persons. Specifically:

- Systematic and extensive evaluation/profiling with legal or significantly affecting decisions.
- Large-scale processing of special categories (Article 9) or criminal conviction data (Article 10).
- Systematic monitoring of a publicly accessible area on a large scale.

DPAs must publish a list of processing operations requiring DPIAs (and may publish a list of operations not requiring DPIAs).

**DPIA content must include**:
- Systematic description of envisaged processing operations and purposes.
- Assessment of necessity and proportionality.
- Assessment of risks to rights and freedoms of data subjects.
- Measures envisaged to address risks, including safeguards, security measures, and mechanisms to ensure protection and demonstrate compliance (taking into account data subjects' and other relevant persons' rights and legitimate interests).

Where a DPIA indicates high residual risk, the controller must consult the supervisory authority **before** commencing processing (Article 36).

---

## Article 37 — Designation of Data Protection Officer

**Mandatory for**:
- (a) Public authorities/bodies (except courts acting in judicial capacity).
- (b) Controllers/processors whose core activities consist of processing operations which require regular and systematic monitoring of data subjects on a large scale.
- (c) Controllers/processors whose core activities consist of large-scale processing of special categories (Article 9) or criminal conviction data (Article 10).

**DPO requirements**:
- Expert knowledge of data protection law and practices.
- Published contact details; notified to supervisory authority.
- Not to receive instructions regarding performance of DPO tasks.
- Report directly to highest level of management.
- Can be staff member or external service contractor.
- Must be provided with resources necessary to maintain expert knowledge.

---

## Articles 44–49 — International Transfers

Transfers to third countries only where:
- **Adequacy decision** exists for that country (Commission has determined it provides essentially equivalent protection). As of August 2025: UK, Switzerland, Israel, Japan, South Korea, Canada (commercial), New Zealand, Argentina, Uruguay, and others.
- **Appropriate safeguards**: Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs), Binding Corporate Rules, codes of conduct, certification mechanisms, ad hoc clauses authorised by supervisory authority.
- **Derogations**: Explicit consent (informed of risks); necessary for contract; necessary for public interest; necessary for legal claims; vital interests; from public register; compelling legitimate interests (narrow, limited use).

---

## Article 83 — General Conditions for Imposing Administrative Fines

**Tier 1 (most serious)** — up to **€20 million or 4% of global annual turnover** (whichever is higher):
- Violations of: basic principles (Article 5); conditions for consent (Articles 6, 7); children's consent (Article 8); special categories (Article 9); data subjects' rights (Articles 15–22); international transfers (Articles 44–49); supervisory authority orders; etc.

**Tier 2** — up to **€10 million or 2% of global annual turnover** (whichever is higher):
- Violations of: privacy by design/default (Article 25); controller/processor obligations; processor obligations; DPO requirements; certification bodies; notification obligations.

**Relevant factors in determining the fine**:
- Nature, gravity, duration of violation.
- Intentional or negligent character.
- Actions taken to mitigate damage suffered.
- Degree of responsibility.
- Prior relevant infringements.
- Degree of cooperation with the supervisory authority.
- Categories of personal data affected (note: special categories and children's data = higher risk).
- Manner in which supervisory authority became aware.
- Whether measures ordered under Article 58(2) were previously imposed.
- Adherence to approved codes of conduct or certification.
