## Overview

COPPA was enacted in 1998 and the FTC's implementing rule came into force in 2000. The rule was significantly updated in 2013 to expand the definition of personal information and clarify obligations. The FTC proposed further amendments in January 2024 (still pending final rule as of August 2025 cutoff — verify current status).

COPPA applies to:
1. Operators of websites or online services **directed to children** (objective test based on subject matter, visual content, use of animated characters, music, celebrities who appeal to children, etc.).
2. Operators of general-audience websites or services with **actual knowledge** that they are collecting personal information from a child under 13.

Mixed-audience sites (some sections directed to children, some not) may limit COPPA obligations to the child-directed portions if they do not collect personal information before an age screen.

## Definitions (16 C.F.R. § 312.2)

**Child**: Natural person under the age of 13.

**Operator**: Any person who operates a website or online service for commercial purposes, or operates a website or online service and collects or maintains personal information from or about users of such website/service, or on whose behalf such information is collected/maintained, where the website/service is directed to children, or the person has actual knowledge that it is collecting personal information from a child.

**Personal information** (as amended 2013):
- First and last name.
- Home or physical address.
- Online contact information (email address, instant message identifier, screen name revealing online contact information).
- Telephone number.
- Social security number.
- **Persistent identifier** that can be used to recognise a user over time and across different websites/services (e.g., cookie, browser cache, customer number, unique device identifier, IP address, processor or device serial number, unique device identifier).
- **Photo, video, or audio file** that contains a child's image or voice.
- **Geolocation information** sufficient to identify street name and city or town.
- Any information concerning the child or the parents collected online that is combined with any of the above.

**Website or online service directed to children**: Determined by considering: subject matter; visual content; use of animated characters or child-oriented activities and incentives; music or other audio content; age of models; presence of child celebrities/celebrities who appeal to children; language or other characteristics of the website or online service; and whether advertising promoting or appearing on the website/service is directed to children.

**Disclosure**: The release of personal information in any manner, including making it publicly available, selling it, or sharing it.

## Core Obligations

### 1. Privacy Notice

**Homepage/general audience privacy notice** (§ 312.4(b)): Must post a clear, prominent link to the privacy notice on the home page of the website/online service and at each area of the site/service where personal information is collected from children. The link must be labeled clearly (e.g., "Privacy Policy").

**Privacy notice at point of collection** (§ 312.4(b)): A link to the privacy notice (or the notice itself) must appear at each point of collection of personal information from children.

**Content of privacy notice** (§ 312.4(d)): Must include:
- Name and contact information (address, telephone number, email) of all operators collecting or maintaining personal information from children through the site/service.
- Description of types of personal information collected from children, and how collected (directly or passively through tracking technologies).
- Description of how the information is used.
- Whether information is disclosed to third parties, and if so: categories of third parties, the purposes for disclosure, and that the operator has no control over third parties' practices.
- That the operator cannot require a child to disclose more information than is reasonably necessary to participate in the activity.
- That the parent can review/delete the child's personal information and refuse to permit further collection/use; how to exercise those rights.

---

### 2. Verifiable Parental Consent (VPC)

VPC is required **before** an operator:
- Collects personal information from a child.
- Uses personal information from a child.
- Discloses personal information from a child.

**Acceptable VPC methods** (§ 312.5(b)):
- Signing and returning a consent form (via mail, fax, or electronic scan).
- Using a credit card, debit card, or other online payment system where notification of each transaction is sent to the account holder.
- Calling a toll-free number staffed by trained personnel.
- Video conference with trained personnel.
- Government-issued ID (verified against a database, then deleted).
- Facial recognition technology using a government-issued ID (a newer method permitted by FTC).
- A signed statement from a parent with a verification mechanism (e.g., asking for the last four digits of a government ID number — not the full number).
- Knowledge-based authentication (KBA) — dynamic questions that the parent is likely to know but a child unlikely to guess.
- Email plus additional steps (e.g., delayed collection, confirmation email with opt-out mechanism) — **lower standard, only for internal use of data and not disclosure to third parties** (the "email plus" method).

**Exception — internal operations** (§ 312.5(c)): VPC is not required where the operator collects a child's online contact information solely to respond once to a specific request from the child, if the information is not used to recontact the child or for any other purpose, and is not disclosed.

**Exception — support for internal operations** (§ 312.5(c)(7)): Operators may collect persistent identifiers (e.g., for analytics/security) without VPC if the identifier is used only to support internal operations of the website/service and is not used to track children across third-party services, contact children outside the service, behaviorally advertise to children, or build profiles.

---

### 3. Parental Rights

After obtaining VPC, parents retain the right to (§ 312.6):
- **Review** the personal information collected from their child.
- **Delete** the personal information collected from their child.
- **Refuse** to permit the operator's further use or collection of personal information from the child.
- Revoke consent and require deletion of all information — operators must comply, unless they have a legitimate reason to retain (e.g., legal obligation).

Operators must provide a mechanism for parents to exercise these rights.

---

### 4. Data Security and Retention (§ 312.8)

Operators must:
- Establish and maintain **reasonable procedures** to protect the confidentiality, security, and integrity of personal information collected from children.
- Take reasonable steps to **release personal information only to service providers and third parties** capable of maintaining its confidentiality and security.
- Retain personal information collected from a child **only as long as is reasonably necessary** to fulfill the purpose for which it was collected.
- **Delete** personal information using reasonable measures to protect against unauthorised access or use during disposal.

---

### 5. Prohibition on Conditioning Participation (§ 312.7)

Operators must not condition a child's participation in an activity on the child disclosing more personal information than is reasonably necessary to participate in that activity.

## Safe Harbor Programs (§ 312.11)

Industry groups may submit self-regulatory guidelines to the FTC for approval as COPPA safe harbors. Operators that comply with an approved safe harbor program are deemed to be in compliance with COPPA's requirements that the program covers.

Approved safe harbor programs (as of August 2025 cutoff — verify current list with FTC):
- kidSAFE Seal Program
- Entertainment Software Rating Board (ESRB) Privacy Certified
- TRUSTe Children's Program
- Aristotle
- PRIVO

## FTC Enforcement

The FTC enforces COPPA under its Section 5 authority (unfair or deceptive acts or practices). The FTC can bring civil actions for injunctions and civil penalties.

**Civil penalties**: Up to ~$51,744 per violation per day (as of early 2024, subject to annual inflation adjustment). In practice, settlements have ranged from small amounts to landmark cases:
- **TikTok/Musical.ly (2019)**: $5.7 million (then-record civil penalty).
- **YouTube/Google (2019)**: $170 million settlement.
- **Epic Games/Fortnite (2022)**: $275 million COPPA component (as part of larger settlement).
- **Amazon Alexa (2023)**: $25 million.

State attorneys general may also bring enforcement actions in federal court.

## 2024 Proposed COPPA Rule Amendments (Verify Final Status)

The FTC issued an NPRM in January 2024 proposing significant amendments. Key proposed changes:
- Restricting use of children's personal information for advertising purposes.
- Strengthening data retention and deletion obligations.
- Conditioning safe harbor program approval on additional requirements.
- Eliminating the email plus VPC method for most purposes.
- New requirements around data security and school-related operators.
- Clarifying that push notifications and alerts constitute online contact with children.

**Status as of August 2025 cutoff**: Final rule had not yet been published — verify current status at ftc.gov.

## State Laws Overview

COPPA establishes a federal floor. Several US states have enacted or proposed additional protections:

### California — Age-Appropriate Design Code Act (CAADCA / AB 2273)
Modelled on the UK Children's Code. Effective January 2024 (implementation phased). Applies to online services "likely to be accessed by children" under 18. Requires DPIAs, default high-privacy settings, restrictions on profiling children, prohibition on geolocation data collection without clear notice. **Note**: A US district court issued an injunction in 2023 blocking enforcement on First Amendment grounds (NetChoice v. Bonta). Appeal proceedings underway — verify current enforcement status.

### California — Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA/CPRA) — Children's Provisions
Under CCPA, personal information of children under 16 cannot be sold/shared unless the child (if 13–15) or a parent/guardian (if under 13) has affirmatively opted in ("right to opt-in"). Penalties of $7,500 per intentional violation involving minors.

### New York — Child Data Protection Act (A8049-B)
Proposed legislation to restrict data collection and sharing by apps and services used by minors. Verify current legislative status.

### Texas — Securing Children Online through Parental Empowerment Act (SCOPE Act, HB 18)
Enacted 2023. Requires social media platforms to obtain parental consent before allowing children under 18 to create accounts; requires age verification; restricts certain features for minors. Enforcement subject to legal challenges — verify status.

### Florida — HB 3 (signed March 2024)
Bans social media accounts for children under 14; requires parental consent for 14–15 year olds. Enforcement status subject to legal challenges — verify.

### Utah — Social Media Regulation Act
Requires parental consent for minors' accounts; establishes curfew hours for minor social media use; age verification requirements. Verify current enforcement status following legal challenges.

## Practical Notes for International Platforms

Platforms based outside the US but accessible to US children must comply with COPPA if they:
- Knowingly collect personal information from children under 13 in the US; or
- Operate sites or services directed to children (by the objective "directed to" test).

The FTC has taken enforcement action against non-US companies. Practical steps for international platforms:
- Implement an age gate at account creation.
- If COPPA-covered: implement VPC workflow before collecting any personal information from US users identified as under 13.
- Consider a "US children's version" with restricted data practices.
- Review all third-party SDKs, advertising networks, and analytics tools for COPPA compliance (operators are liable for third parties to whom they disclose children's data).
- Ensure parental review and deletion mechanisms are functional and clearly disclosed.
