## Overview

The Online Safety Act 2023 (OSA 2023) is the primary UK legislation governing the safety of internet services. It places a suite of duties on providers of regulated user-to-user services (platforms where users can interact with each other's content) and search services that are accessible to UK users. The Act establishes a risk-based framework: the more harmful a service could be, and the more likely children are to access it, the more stringent the obligations.

Ofcom is the designated regulator. It issues Codes of Practice and guidance that providers must have regard to when fulfilling their duties.

---

## Part 1 — Introductory Provisions

### Key Definitions

**Regulated service** (s.2–3): A "user-to-user service" or a "search service" that has a nexus to the United Kingdom — i.e., it has a significant number of UK users, targets UK users, or UK users form a material part of the user base.

**User-to-user service** (s.3): An internet service by means of which content that is generated, uploaded, or shared by a user of the service may be encountered by another user. Examples: social media platforms, forums, video-sharing platforms, dating apps, messaging services (where content is publicly or semi-publicly visible).

**Search service** (s.5): An internet service that enables users to search a database of information (including content from across the internet).

**Exemptions** (Schedule 1): Internal business services, services with no UK users, one-to-one live communications only (pure one-to-one calls not combined with a user-to-user element), limited-functionality email and SMS services, certain news publisher services.

**Children** (s.2): Individuals under 18 years of age.

**Illegal content** (s.52): Content that amounts to, or is generated by, a criminal offence under UK law, or certain specified priority offences.

**Priority illegal content** (Schedule 5): Terrorism content, CSAM (child sexual abuse material), hate crime content, harassment, controlling/coercive behaviour, drugs/weapons supply, fraud and financial crime, and others.

**Content that is harmful to children** (s.12): Content of a kind that Ofcom has designated as "primary priority content" or "priority content" in codes of practice, and other content that the provider reasonably considers to be harmful to children.

**Primary priority content harmful to children** (Schedule 6): Content promoting suicide or self-harm; content depicting serious violence; content that is pornographic.

**Priority content harmful to children** (Schedule 7): Content that bullies or harasses; content promoting eating disorders; content promoting dangerous activities; etc.

## Part 2 — Provider Duties (General Framework)

### Category 1, 2A, and 2B Services

Ofcom will categorise regulated services by size and risk (s.95):

- **Category 1**: Largest user-to-user services (high reach + high-risk features). Subject to the full suite of duties including transparency reporting, user empowerment tools, and content moderation obligations.
- **Category 2A**: Search services meeting a threshold. Subject to search-specific duties.
- **Category 2B**: User-to-user services with a lower threshold (large but not Category 1). Subject to a lighter set of additional duties.

### Illegal Content Duties (s.9–10, all regulated services)

All providers of regulated services must:

1. Conduct and document a **illegal content risk assessment** before making the service available, and review it when there are material changes.
2. Implement **proportionate systems and processes** to:
   - Prevent individuals from encountering priority illegal content.
   - Minimise the time illegal content (including priority illegal content) is present.
   - Swiftly remove illegal content when detected or reported.
3. Operate a **complaints mechanism** allowing users to report illegal content.
4. Act in accordance with their own **terms of service** in relation to illegal content.

### Children's Safety Duties (s.11–12, all regulated services)

Where a service is "likely to be accessed by children" (assessed objectively — see below), providers must:

1. Conduct a **children's risk assessment** identifying how and to what extent children may encounter primary priority or priority harmful content.
2. Implement **safety measures** proportionate to the identified risk.
3. Use **highly effective age assurance** to prevent children from encountering the most harmful content (primary priority content harmful to children).
4. Not algorithmically recommend primary priority content harmful to children to any user.

**"Likely to be accessed by children"**: Ofcom will publish guidance. The test is objective — providers cannot escape the duty merely by prohibiting children in their terms. Factors include: subject matter, visual content, language, prevalence of children on similar services, whether the service has age-verification.

### Content Moderation Duties (Category 1 services)

Category 1 providers must additionally:

- Offer **user empowerment tools**: allow adult users to control their exposure to legal but harmful content.
- Operate **systems to protect adults** from legal but potentially harmful content.
- Publish and enforce **terms of service** transparently and consistently.
- Not carry out **discriminatory treatment** of certain news publishers' content.

## Part 3 — Children's Safety Duties in Detail

### Risk Assessment Obligations

**s.11**: Every provider of a regulated service that is likely to be accessed by children must conduct a children's risk assessment that:

- Identifies the kinds of content harmful to children that children are likely to encounter on the service.
- Assesses the level of risk of children of different ages encountering such content.
- Assesses the likelihood that the service will be accessed by children (taking into account any age assurance measures already in place).
- Reviews and updates the assessment when material changes occur.

### Safety Measures

Where the risk assessment identifies a risk that children may encounter primary priority content harmful to children, providers must:

- Implement **highly effective age assurance** measures so that children are not normally able to access that content.
- Ensure that algorithms do not recommend such content to children or to unverified users (where the content could be accessed by children).

Where the risk is of encountering priority content harmful to children (the second tier), providers must implement proportionate measures to reduce the risk to an acceptable level.

### Age Assurance Definition

**"Highly effective age assurance"** (s.15): Age assurance is highly effective if it is highly likely to prevent children from accessing the relevant part of the service. Ofcom will specify in Codes of Practice what methods satisfy this standard. Methods may include:

- Photo ID matching
- Credit card checks
- Digital identity verification
- Device-based age estimation
- Third-party age verification services

Age assurance must not be solely based on a user self-declaring their age.

## Part 4 — Age Assurance Requirements

The Act uses two concepts:

1. **Age assurance** (broadly): any technical or procedural means of estimating or verifying a user's age.
2. **Highly effective age assurance**: the higher standard required to prevent children from accessing primary priority harmful content.

Ofcom's Codes of Practice will specify approved methodologies. The Code of Practice on Children's Safety (consulted 2024) is the key reference.

**Key principles**:
- Age assurance must be **proportionate to the risk** and the nature of the content.
- It must be **privacy-preserving** where possible — providers should not require excessive data collection purely to comply.
- Providers may use **age estimation technology** (which does not require identity verification) for lower-risk content.
- The Act does not mandate any single method.

## Part 5 — Content Reporting, Complaints and Appeals

### User Reporting (s.19)

All regulated services must provide **accessible, transparent, and easy-to-use mechanisms** for users to:
- Report illegal content.
- Report content that is harmful to children (where applicable).
- Report breaches of the provider's terms of service.

### Complaints Mechanism (s.20)

Providers must operate a complaints mechanism that:
- Acknowledges complaints promptly.
- Reviews and adjudicates complaints fairly.
- Notifies complainants of outcomes.
- Handles appeals against content removal decisions.

### Category 1 Super-Complaints

Super-complaints may be made by approved bodies (representing users or classes of users) to Ofcom where there is a systemic failure by a Category 1 provider.

## Part 6 — Ofcom's Powers and Enforcement

### Information Powers (s.101–108)

Ofcom may issue **information notices** requiring providers to supply information relevant to Ofcom's functions. Failure to comply, or providing false information, is a criminal offence for the provider and potentially for senior managers.

### Audit Powers (s.109)

Ofcom may require providers to commission and submit **independent audits** of their compliance with the Act's duties.

### Enforcement Notices (s.110–116)

Where Ofcom reasonably believes a provider is failing to comply with a duty, it may:
1. Issue a **provisional notice of contravention** with findings.
2. Allow the provider to make representations.
3. Issue a **confirmation decision** requiring remediation steps.

### Financial Penalties (s.117–120)

| Contravention | Maximum penalty |
|---|---|
| Failure to comply with safety duties (user-to-user / search) | £18 million or 10% of qualifying worldwide revenue |
| Failure to comply with information notices | £18 million or 10% of qualifying worldwide revenue |
| Providing false information | Up to 2 years' imprisonment (senior manager) |

### Business Disruption Measures (s.121–131)

For serious or persistent non-compliance, Ofcom can apply to court for orders requiring:
- **Payment service providers** to stop processing payments for the service.
- **App stores** to cease distributing the service's app.
- **Internet access providers** to block access to the service (ISP blocking — a last resort).

### Senior Manager Liability (s.179–181)

An individual designated as a **Senior Manager** of a non-compliant provider can be criminally liable where:
- Ofcom has issued an enforcement notice.
- The individual failed to take all reasonable steps to secure compliance.
- The failure was deliberate or negligent.

## Schedule 5 — Priority Illegal Content

Content amounting to (or generated by committing):
- Terrorism offences (Terrorism Act 2000, 2006)
- Child sexual abuse material (Sexual Offences Act 2003; Protection of Children Act 1978)
- Hate crime offences (Public Order Act 1986; Crime and Disorder Act 1998)
- Harassment offences (Protection from Harassment Act 1997)
- Controlling or coercive behaviour (Serious Crime Act 2015, s.76)
- Drugs/weapons supply offences
- Fraud and financial crime (Fraud Act 2006)
- Human trafficking (Modern Slavery Act 2015)
- Encouraging or assisting suicide (Suicide Act 1961, s.2)
- Intimate image abuse (non-consensual sharing of sexual images)
- Foreign interference offences (National Security Act 2023)

---

## Schedule 6 — Primary Priority Content Harmful to Children

- Content that encourages, promotes, or provides instructions for suicide or self-harm.
- Content that is pornographic.
- Content depicting or encouraging serious violence.

---

## Schedule 7 — Priority Content Harmful to Children

- Content that bullies or harasses a child.
- Content that encourages, promotes, or provides instructions for eating disorders.
- Content that promotes or encourages dangerous challenges or stunts.
- Content that promotes or facilitates grooming of children.
- Content promoting or facilitating child sexual exploitation.
- Violent content not covered in Schedule 6.

## Compliance Notes for Live Video / Creator Platforms

- A platform where creators broadcast live to audiences of fans, and where fans can post comments or interact, is almost certainly a **user-to-user service**.
- If any live streams or recorded content could be accessed by individuals under 18, the **children's safety duties** are engaged.
- **Age assurance** will be required if the platform hosts any primary priority content (e.g., sexual content) — even if creator accounts are restricted to adults.
- **Complaints and reporting** mechanisms must be in place from day one of operation.
- Ofcom's **Children's Safety Code of Practice** (in development as of August 2025) will be the primary operational guide.
